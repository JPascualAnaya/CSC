Biology behind the ConSite algorithm

Computational detection of transcription factor binding sites in genomic sequence is difficult due to the limited binding specificity of most transcription factors, as evidenced in a high ratio of false to true predictions.

One approach of growing popularity is based on the preferential conservation of functional sequences over the course of evolution by selective pressure; termed "phylogenetic footprinting" . Mutations are more likely to be disruptive if they appear in functional sites, resulting in a measurable difference in the rate of evolution between functional and non-functional segments in the genome. The ConSite system reports those putative transcription factor binding sites both situated in conserved regions and located as pairs of sites in equivalent positions in alignments between two orthologous sequences. This approach results in a significant improvement of transcription factor binding sites detection due to an increased signal/noise ratio(Figure 1).

Evolutionary filtering and evolutionary distance

It should be noted that evolutionary rates vary dramatically between genes and the choice of species is an important consideration in "phylogenetic footprinting" studies (Figure 1). Too great an evolutionary distance can result in regulatory alterations or difficulty in aligning short patches of similarity between long sequences. Inadequate evolutionary distance does not significantly improve the overall specificity of predictions

Examples of ConSite analysis on non-mammalien species

Comparison of genomic sequences from related species is an effective method for the identification of segments likely to mediate a specific biological function. While the work presented in our paper (submitted) focuses on mammalian sequence comparisons, there is no limitation within the ConSite system precluding studies of other organisms.

rVISTA -ConSite comparison

 

Test set-up

rVISTA was subject to the same testing procedure and data as ConSite (described in manuscript).

Worth consideration is that rVISTA is given several advantages during this test:

Circularity

It is not clarified wether the TRANSFAC matrix collection is independent from the sites we search for. Thereby, rVISTA has an advantage due to circularity in the testing procedure.

Redundancy

In addition, rVISTA includes a redundant matrix collection, thus they have multiple models for several of the transcription factors. In our experience with the Transfac database, many of these models are overly specific (and sensitive to circularity problems) as they are built with only a few sites

Evolutionary distance measurements

The available test sets are ill-suited to explore greater evolutionary distances. In greater evolutionary distances, the alignment algorithms probably favor ConSite even further, as VISTA is optimized for rapid alignment of more similar pairs of sequences. ConSite also allows users to submit alignments from any algorithm through a standard format (ClustalW), which makes it more flexible for future improvements in alignment methods.

 

Sensitivity testing

In essence, the sensitivity is measured by analyzing how many of the known sites in the set that are found using the appropriate models. Since there is no possibility for users to view matrix models within rVISTA, identification of appropriatee matrix models was based on matrix names and biological knowledge.

At 70% matrix threshold and 70% conservation cutoff, (all other settings default), ConSite locates 62.5% of the true sites, while rVISTA only can find 52.5%.

 

Selectivity

We measure how well the orthologous sequence analysis can filter out false positives compared to single sequence analysis. The total number of predictions using respective full matrix collection is used as an approximation of the number of false positives, since most predicted sites are not biologically relevant. There is no functionality for single sequence comparison within the rVISTA system, instead we have aligned two identical sequences and used that as input. Since rVISTA only uses the other sequence for identifying regions of conservation, the results should be identical to single sequence analysis.

The total number of predicted sites using orthologous analysis divided by the same results using a single sequence is used as a measure of the filtering efficiency. Since this is an internal test, differences in matrix collection size are not an issue.

At the same settings as above, the ratio for ConSite is 13.6% , implying that about 87% of the noise is removed in the anlysis.

The corresponding test for rVISTA generates a ratio of 16.6%.

It is also worth pointing out that rVISTA, partly due to the redundancy and low information content models in their matrix collection generates, on average, 4-5 as many predicted sites as ConSite using any setting.

Conclusion

User friendliness and functionality aside, ConSite still outperforms rVISTA in both sensitivity and selectivity, despite that rVISTA is given several advantages during the test.
